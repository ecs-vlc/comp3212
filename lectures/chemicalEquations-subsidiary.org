#+TITLE: Computational Biology Subsidary Notes
#+SUBTITLE: Lecture 18: Simulating Chemical Equations

* Keywords
  * Gene regulation, chemical equations, motifs

* Main Points

** Stochasticity
   - Chemical equations happen by chance interactions of molecules
     + The molecules are moving around due to Brownian motion and when
       the right molecules collide with the right energy a reaction happens
   - If there are enough molecules then the actual number of molecules
     will be close to their mean number and the behaviour is well
     described by ordinary differential equations
   - In some biological situations there may be so few molecules of a
     specific type that the stochastic nature of the dynamics qualitatively
     changes what happens
   - There is considerable research interest as to whether cells can
     use this stochasticity for their own purposes
     + For example, in a population of cells you might want some
       proportion of cells to respond to a stimulus or to
       differentiate
     + This can be achieved if the state of the cell is controlled by
       a stochastic process caused by the existence of a very small
       number of molecules
   - In modelling stochastic behaviour biologists often distinguish
     between two different types of randomness
     1. *Extrinsic Noise*
	* This is noise that is external to the chemical equations
          being modelled
	* This might arise due to changes in the number of RNA-polymerase
          or ribosomes, or the size of cells
     2. *Intrinsic Noise*
	* This is randomness that arises because of stochasticity in
          the chemical processes being modelled

*** Probability of a Reaction
    - When modelling particular reactions we have to calculate the
      probability of the reactants to come together
    - Consider the reaction
      \[  2\,X + Y \goesto{k} 3\, X \]
    - We assume that this reaction will only take place if two $X$
      molecules and one $Y$ molecule co-exist in a small volume
      $\delta V$
      #+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.25\textwidth
       [[./figures/smallVolume.pdf]]
    - The probability that a particular molecule $Y_i$ exists in a
      small volume, $\delta V$, is given by $\Prob{Y_i\in\delta V} = \delta
      V/V$, where $V$ is the volume of the cell
    - If there are $n_Y$ molecules of type $Y$ in the cell, then the
      probability of there being at least one molecule in volume
      $\delta V$ is $n_Y\,\delta V/V = [Y]\,\delta V$ (where $[Y] =
      n_Y/V$ is the concentration of $Y$)
    - The probability that any two particles $X_1$ and $X_2$ say are
      both in volume $V$ at any one time is $(\delta V/V)^2$
    - If there are $n_X$ particles of type $X$ then the probability
      that at least two of them are in volume $\delta V$ is
      \[ \frac{m\,(m-1)}{2} \left( \frac{\delta V}{V} \right)^2
      \approx \frac{(\delta V)^2}{2} [X]^2 \]
    - The probability of at least one particle of type $Y$ and two
      particles of type $X$ are in volume $\delta V$ is
      \[ \frac{(\delta V)^3}{2} \,[Y]\,[X]^2  \]
    - There are $V/(\delta V)$ volumes of size $\delta V$ in our cell
      of volume $V$
    - Thus the probability that in one of these volumes there are two
      molecules of type $X$ and one of type $Y$ is
      \[ \frac{V\,(\delta V)^2}{2} \,[Y]\,[X]^2  \]
    - The reaction rate $k$ of this reaction is defined so that the
      probability of there being a reaction in time $\delta t$ per
      unit volume is
      \[ k\,[Y]\,[X]^2 \, \delta t\]
      + Note that $k$ is defined to absorb the combinatorial function $\tfrac{1}{2}$
      + The rate will depend on how close together the molecules have
        to be for a reaction to occur (i.e. on $\delta V$)
      + There will be other factors such as the orientation of the
        molecules and the relative velocity of the molecules
      + From a chemical point of view, $k$, would measure the reaction
        rate per litre if all the reactants were 1 molar solutions (1
        moles of $X$ and $Y$ per litre (dm$^{3}$))
    - To model small numbers of molecules we have to work with the
      number of molecules $n_X$, $n_Y$, etc.
    - We note that $[X] = n_X/(N_A\,V)$ where $N_A$ is Avogadro's
      number and $V$ is the volume of the cell (we frequently write
      $\Omega = N_A\,V$)
    - If we have a reaction $2\,X + Y \goesto{k} 3\, X$
      then the change in concentration per unit time is $k\, [Y]\,[X]^2$
    - Multiply this rate by $\Omega = N_A\,V$ we get the expected number of
      reactions in a cell of volume $V$ per unit time
    - Thus the probability of this reaction occurring in time $\delta t$ is
      \[ \frac{k}{\Omega^2} \, n_Y \, n_X^2 \, \delta t \]
    - If we wanted to be more precise (which might be important if we
      have a very small number of $X$ molecules) then this probability
      is
       \[ \frac{k}{\Omega^2} \, n_Y \, n_X\,(n_X-1)\, \delta t \]

** Gillespie Algorithm
   - We can simulate dynamics of a molecular system when we
     have a small number of molecules
     + This is just one instance of what might happen (next time we do
       the simulation we would get a different dynamics)
   - Let's consider the system
    \begin{align*}
      \varnothing &\goesto{k_1} A  &
      \varnothing &\goesto{k_2} B & 
       A + B &\goesto{k_3} C &
       C &\goesto{k_4} A + B
    \end{align*}
   - We let $n_A(t)$, $n_B(t)$ and $n_C(t)$ be the number of molecules
     of type $A$, $B$ and $C$ at time $t$
   - We are going to assume that the reactions are so fast that they happen instantly
   - In the theory of stochastic processes these are known as *point processes*
   - Now the probability of a reaction occurring it time $\delta t$ is
     \begin{align*}
     \Prob{\varnothing \goesto{k_1} A} &= k_1\,\delta t  = a_1(\bm{X}(t))\, \delta t\\
     \Prob{\varnothing \goesto{k_2} B} &= k_2\,\delta t = a_2(\bm{X}(t))\, \delta t \\
     \Prob{A + B \goesto{k_3} C} &= \frac{k_3}{\Omega}\,n_{A}\,n_{B}\,\delta t = a_3(\bm{X}(t))\, \delta t \\
     \Prob{C \goesto{k_4} A + B} &= k_4\,n_C\,\delta t  = a_4(\bm{X}(t))\, \delta t
     \end{align*}
   - $a_i(\bm{X}(t))$ are known as the *propensity* of reaction $i$
   - Many of the propensity will change after each reaction
   - *Gillespie's algorithm* simulates a chemical process one reaction
     at a time
     + From the propensities it draws a random time interval, $T$, to
       the next chemical reaction
     + We then randomly decides which chemical reaction takes place
     + We then updates the propensities
   - What is surprising is just how easy it is to simulate chemical
     equations using Gillespie's algorithm

*** Poisson Process
    - In modelling chemical equations each reaction happens
      independently at each time interval
    - As a consequence our point process becomes a *Poisson process*
      (the simplest type of point process)
    - We suppose we have a point process with a probability of occurring
      in time $\delta t$ of $a\, \delta t$
    - The probability of the first occurrence happening in time interval
      $n\,\delta t \leq t \leq (n+1)\,\delta t$ is
      \begin{align*}
      \Prob{n\,\delta t \leq t \leq (n+1)\,\delta t}
      &\eq (1-a \,\delta t)^n \,a \, \delta\,t
      \eq a \, \e{n\log(1-a \,\delta t)} \, \delta t\\
      &\eq[\approx] a \, \e{-a\,n \,\delta t} \, \delta t
      \eq[\approx] a \, \e{-a\,t} \, \delta t
      \end{align*}
      \explanation
      1) This is the probability that it doesn't happen, $1-a\,\delta
	 t$, in the previous $n$ time intervals times the probability
	 that it does happen, $a\,\delta t$
      2) Using $x^n = \e{n\,\log(x)}$
      3) Since $\log(1+\Delta) \approx \Delta +O(\Delta^2)$
      4) Using $n\,\delta t \approx t$
    - Taking the limit $\delta t\rightarrow 0$ then the probability
      density for the next time an event occurs is
      \begin{align*}
      f_T(t) = \lim_{\delta t\rightarrow 0} \frac{\Prob{n\,\delta
      t \leq t \leq (n+1)\,\delta t}}{\delta t}= a\,\e{-a\,t}
      \end{align*}
    - In modelling a series of $K$ chemical equations where the
      probability of reaction $i$ occurring in time $\delta t$ is
      $a_i\,\delta t$ then the probability of any one reaction
      occurring in this time interval is $a_0 \,\delta t$ where
      $a_0=\sum_{i=1}^K a_i$
    - If $T$ is a random variable equal to the time until the next
      reaction then it will be distributed according to the probability
      density
      \[ f_T(t) = a_0\,\e{-a_0\,t} \]
    - The probability that when a reaction happens
      that it is reaction $i$ is
      \begin{align*}
      \Prob{\text{reaction $i$ happens}} =
      \frac{a_i}{a_1+\cdots+a_K} = \frac{a_i}{a_0}
      \end{align*}
    - We now need to be able draw random deviates from an exponential distribution
    - We first consider the case when $a_{0}=1$ so that $f_T(t) =
      \mathrm{Exp}(t) = \e{-t}$
    - The trick to drawing a random deviate from this distribution is
      to invert the cumulative probability distribution
      \[ F_T(t) = \int_0^t \, \mathrm{Exp}(\tau) \, \dd \tau =
      1-\e{-t} \]
    - So that $F^{-1}_T(x) = -\log(1-x)$
    - Now if we choose a uniform random deviate $U\sim
      \mathrm{Uni}(0,1)$ between 0 and 1 then
      \[ T = F^{-1}_T(U) \]
      will be exponentially distributed
      + We can prove this, but this goes beyond what you need to know
        for the course
      + Let's consider the probability that $T$ is between $t$ and
        $t + \delta t$
	\[ \Prob{t\leq T \leq t+\delta t} = \int_0^1 \pred{t\leq
        F^{-1}_T(u)\leq t +\delta t}\, \dd u \]
      + where $\pred{\text{predicate}} = 1$ if /predicate/ is true and
        0 otherwise
      + But we can rewrite this as
	\[ \Prob{t\leq T \leq t+\delta t} = \int_{F_T(t)}^{F_T(t+\delta
        t)} \,\dd u = F_T(t+\delta t) - F_T(t) \]
	* You can convince yourself that for $u\in[F_T(t),F_T(t+\delta
          t)]}$ we have $t\leq F_T^{-1}(u)\leq t + \tau$
      + Using the Taylor expansion $F_T(t+\delta t)  = F_T(t) + \delta t
        \, F_{T}'(t) + O(\delta t^2)$
      + But \(F_T'(t) = f_T(t)\) that is, the derivative of a cumulative
        distribution function (CDF) is equal to the probability
        density function (PDF)
      + Thus $\Prob{t\leq T \leq t+\delta t}/\delta t = f_{T}(t) +
        O(\delta t)$
      + Taking the limit when $\delta t\rightarrow0$ we see that $T$
        has a probability density $f_T(t)$
    - To draw a random deviate $T \sim \mathrm{Exp}(t|a) =
       a\,\exp(-a\,t)$ we just make the change of variable from $T$ to $T/a$
    - That is, we choose $T = -\log(1-U)/a$
    - For the Gillespie algorithm with molecule species $\bm{X}(t) =
      (X_1(t),\,X_2(t),\,\ldots,\,X_N(t))$ where we have $K$ reactions
      with propensities $a_i(\bm{X}(t))$ we do the following
      1. Compute the total propensity $a_0(\bm{X}(t)) = \sum_{j=1}^K
         a_i(\bm{X}(t))$
      2. Draw a random time $T\sim \mathrm{Exp(t|a_0)}$ using
	 \[ T &= - \frac{1}{a_0(\bm{X}(t))} \log(1-U) & U&\sim\mathrm{Uni}(0,1) \]
      3. Select the reaction that happened with probabilities
	 \[ \Prob{j} = \frac{a_j(\bm{X}(t))}{a_0(\bm{X}(t))} \]
      4. Update time $t\rightarrow t + T$
      5. Update the molecular numbers according to the reaction that
         has taken place
    - For the example we discussed previously
      \begin{align*}
       \varnothing &\goesto{0.05} A  &
       \varnothing &\goesto{0.03} B & 
       A + B &\goesto{0.005/\Omega} C &
       C &\goesto{0.01} A + B
     \end{align*}
      #+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.5\textwidth
       [[./figures/reactions-20.pdf]]
    - As a second example consider the two species system we studied
      in the last lecture
      \begin{align*}
      \varnothing &\goesto{f(X_2)} X_1 &
      X_1 &\goesto{k_3} \varnothing & X_1 &\goesto{k_5} X_2 \\
      \varnothing &\goesto{k_2} X_2 & X_2 &\goesto{k_4} \varnothing &&
      \end{align*}
      + Here $X_2$ represses the expression of $X_1$ according to the
        Hill equation for a repressor
         \begin{align*}
         f(X_2) &= \frac{k_1}{1+(X_2/K)^n}\pause
         \end{align*}
      + Leading to the coupled ODEs
         \begin{align*}
          \frac{\dd [X_1(t)]}{\dd t} &= \frac{k_1}{1+([X_2(t)]/K)^n} - k_3 \,
                                    [X_1(t)]- k_5\,[X_1(t)] \\
           \frac{\dd [X_2(t)]}{\dd t} &= k_2 + k_5\,[X_1(t)] - k_4\,[X_2(t)]
         \end{align*}
      + We can solve the ODEs giving a dynamics for $K=1$, $k_1=20$,
        $k_2=k_3=k_4=5$, $k_5=2$, $n=4$
        #+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.5\textwidth
        [[./figures/coupledODE-41.pdf]]
      + If we change the units of concentration so that $[X_i(t)]
        \rightarrow r\,[X_i(t)]$ this would lead to a  change in the differential equations
      + But if we rescale the reaction rates appropriately $K\rightarrow r\,K$,
        $t\rightarrow r\,t$, $k_i\rightarrow k_i/r$ for
        $i\in\{3,4,5\}$ this leaves the ODEs invariant
      + This allows us to simulate the system with the same mean
        dynamics but with different number of molecules in the finite
        molecule simulation (this is entirely artificial in any real
        biological system the reaction rates and cell volume are given)
      + Using $r=1000$ we can simulate the dynamics as shown in figure \ref{fig:large}
     	 #+Caption: Gillespie dynamics using $r=1000$ \label{fig:large}
	 #+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.5\textwidth
          [[./figures/GillespieLarge-39.pdf]]
      + The dashed curves in figure \ref{fig:large} shows the ODE solution
      + The continuous curve shows the Gillespie dynamics
      + Using this value of $r$ then $K=1000$, $k_1=0.02$,
        $k_2=k_3=k_4=0.005$, $k_5=0.002$ and $n=4$ and the time is in
        measured in units of 1000 second
      + Using $r=100$ we can simulate the dynamics as shown in figure \ref{fig:medium}
     	 #+Caption: Gillespie dynamics using $r=100$ \label{fig:medium}
	 #+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.5\textwidth
          [[./figures/GillespieMedium-39.pdf]]
      + Using $r=10$ we can simulate the dynamics as shown in figure \ref{fig:small}
     	 #+Caption: Gillespie dynamics using $r=10$ \label{fig:small}
	 #+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.5\textwidth
          [[./figures/GillespieSmall-39.pdf]]
      + This shows how when we have a smaller number of molecules the
        ODEs can provide a poor description of what goes on
      + If we have a stable fixed-points with a small basin of attraction often
        these fluctuations mean that the system always escapes from
        this fixed point
      + Occasionally these fluctuations can cause a random
        flip-flopping between different fixed-points
    - Gillespie is very efficient and can simulate systems with a
      millions of molecules
    - However, we can easily have systems where we have too many molecules
      for Gillespie to be feasible (Avogadro's number is very large)
    - Usually this doesn't matter because this is the regime where the
      ODEs work
    - There can be times where some molecules are very common and
      others very rare in which case we often need to use tricks so we
      can simulate the rare molecules using Gillespie, but use some
      other approximation to simulate the other species of molecules
