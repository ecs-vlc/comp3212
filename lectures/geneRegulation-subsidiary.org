* Keywords
  * Evolution, DNA, Proteins

* Main Points

** Gene Regulation Networks
   - We are currently in a phase of science where we are beginning to
     understand how cells regulate themselves
   - A major mechanism is through switching on and off the expression
     of proteins
   - This happens through /transcription factors/ (proteins) which
     + sensing the environment (often through signalling molecules)
     + bind to the promoter regions of DNA
     + excite or inhibit the transcription of RNA for the
       corresponding protein
   - There is a complex networks of interactions making it very
     challenging to understand what is going on

q** Chemical Equations
   - The gene regulation process is a dynamic process
   - Reactions happen on different time scales
     + This helps us: we are usually interested in what happens on the slow
       time scale
     + Fast reactions can be assumed to reach their equilibrium state instantaneously
     + Proteins binding with small (signalling) molecules will happen
       in milliseconds
     + A transcription factor protein will bind with the promoter
       region in a few second
     + The transcription and translation of a gene to a protein takes
       around 5 minutes
     + The change in the concentration of a protein by 50% takes about
       an hour
   - To model the dynamics we start from "/chemical equations/"
     + These are not what chemists would recognise as chemical equations
     + They don't respect conservation of matter
     + This is because they only capture the slow processes and ignore
       fast processes (the equations would be far too complicated to
       be useful if they described everything that is going on)
   - Chemical equations are really useful for getting a handle
     on the dynamics
   - As an example
      \begin{align}
       X &\stackrel{k_1}{\longrightarrow} X + \RNA_Y \label{eq:XRNAY}\\
       \RNA_Y &\stackrel{k_2}{\longrightarrow} \RNA_Y + Y \label{eq:YRNAY}\\
       \RNA_Y &\stackrel{k_3}{\longrightarrow} \varnothing \label{eq:YRNA0}
      \end{align}
     + (\ref{eq:XRNAY}) described a transcription factor, $X$, that excites
       the production of the RNA for gene $Y$ at a rate $k_1$ (this
       is a catalytic reaction that doesn't change $X$)
     + (\ref{eq:YRNAY}) $\RNA_Y$  is  translated to protein $Y$ at a
       rate $k_2$ (this is also a catalytic reaction that does not
       change $\RNA_{Y}$)
     + (\ref{eq:YRNA0}) Describes the breakdown of $RNA_{Y}$ at a rate $k_3$
     + All these equations are shorthand for complex processes
   - The main point of writing down these chemical processes is to
     understand their dynamics
   - Chemical equation happen by chance so their dynamics is
     intrinsically stochastic, but if there are a sufficient number of
     molecules then the number of molecules at anytime will be close
     to their expected number
   - We can model the change in the expected concentrations using a set of first
     order ordinary differential equations
   - We use $[X]$ to denote the concentration of $X$
   - For the chemical equation above
      \begin{align*}
      \frac{\dd [\RNA_Y]}{\dd t} &= k_1 \, [X] - k_3\,  [\RNA_Y] \\
      \frac{\dd [Y]}{\dd t} &= k_2 \, [\RNA_Y]
      \end{align*}
     + We get one equation for every molecular species that we are modelling
     + Each term on the right-hand side $RHS$ corresponds to a reaction
     + The rate of change for a species $S$ is proportional to
       1. The product of concentrations on the LHS of the chemical equations
       2. The reaction rate of the equation
       3. The number of $S$ on the RHS minus the number of $S$ on the LHS
   - As a mad examples
     \begin{align*}
     5 \,X + 2\, Y &\stackrel{k_1}{\longrightarrow} 2\, X + Z \\
     Z + X &\stackrel{k_2}{\longrightarrow} 3\, X
     \end{align*}
     would lead to the ODEs
     \begin{align*}
     \frac{\dd [X]}{\dd t} &= -3\,k_1\, [X]^5 \, [Y]^2 + 2 \, k_2 \, [X]\,[Z] \\
     \frac{\dd [Y]}{\dd t} &= -2\,k_1\, [X]^5 \, [Y]^2 \\
     \frac{\dd [Z]}{\dd t} &= k_{1}\, [X]^5 \, [Y]^2 - k_2 \, [X]\,[Z]
     \end{align*}

*** Biological Switches
    - Chemical equations are non-linear
      + This makes the dynamics complicated (mathematicians only know
        how to solve a very small number of non-linear differential
        equations)
      + From a biological point of view non-linearity allows the cell
        to have more complex dynamics
      + Non-linearities allow us to have faster switching behaviour
    - The Hill equation is a classic model for a biological "switch"
      + We assume that a protein, $X$, will form a relatively stable
        complex $C$ with $n$ signalling molecules $S_X$ (these have to
        come together simultaneously)\vspace*{-1ex}
        \begin{align*}
          n\, S_X + X &\stackrel{k_{on}}{\longrightarrow} C
          &
          C &\stackrel{k_{off}}{\longrightarrow} n\, S_X + X
        \end{align*}
      + The ODE for the concentration of complex $C$ is
	\begin{align*}
	\frac{\dd [C]}{\dd t} = k_{on}\,[X]\,[S_X]^n - k_{off} [C]
        \end{align*}
      + Solving this is difficult, but the fixed point is given by
	\( \displaystyle \frac{\dd [C]}{\dd t} = 0  \)
      + Thus at equilibrium: $k_{off}\,[C] = k_{on}\,[X]\,[S_X]^n$
      + But the sum of $X$ and $X$ molecules never change: $[X]+[C] =[X_T]$
      + Thus $k_{off}\,[C] = k_{on}\,([X_T]-[C])\,[S_X]^n$
      + Rearranging\vspace*{-2ex}
	\begin{align*}
	\frac{k_{off}}{k_{on}}\,[C] = ([X_T]-[C])\,[S_X]^n
       \end{align*}
      + We define $K_X^n = k_{off}/k_{on}$
	* We choose this definition so that $K_X$ has dimensions of concentration
      + Thus: $K_X^n\,[C] = ([X_T]-[C])\,[S_X]^n$
      + Rearranging $(K_X^n + [S_X]^n)\,[C] = [X_T]\,[S_X]^n$
      + Or \vspace*{-2ex}
	\[ \frac{[C]}{[X_T]} = \frac{[S_X]^{n}}{K_X^n+[S_{X}]^n} \]
      + This is the Hill equation
      + In Figure \ref{fig:hill} show the left-hand side versus
        $[S_{X}]$ for different $n$
	#+Caption: Hill Curves for differnt values of $n$ \label{fig:hill}
	#+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.62\textwidth
	[[./figures/hillCurves-4.pdf]]
      + Note that by using $n>1$ we get a much faster switching in the
        concentration of $C$
      + When the protein acts as a transcription factor that increases
        the expression of another protein (a, so called, /activator/),
        then the effective rate of producing the new protein is
	\[ f([S_X]) = \frac{\beta\,[X]^n}{K^n + [S_X]^n} \]
      + When $X$ inhibits the expression of another protein (a, so
        called, /repressor/) then the rate of production of the other
        protein is
	\[ f([S_X]) = \frac{\beta}{1 + ([S_X]/K)^n} \]
	#+Caption: Activator and Repressor \label{fig:activerepress}

	[[./figures/activerepress.pdf]]

** Motifs
   - Trying to make sense of a huge network of interacting proteins is
     a real challenge
   - One common approach is to look at small sub-networks that occur
     more often than we would expect by chance
   - These are called *motifs*
   - Many motifs have been studied, we will consider one example: /negative auto-regulation/

*** Auto-regulation
   - When you look at gene regulation networks (GRN) there are a lot
     of genes that regulate (suppress) there own production
   - To see whether this happens by chance lets look at /E-coli/ (one
     of the most studied organisms on the planet)
     + In /E-coli/ there are $N=424$ proteins with $E=519$ known interactions
       between the proteins
     + 40 of these interactions are self-interactions
     + Could this happen by chance?
     + To calculate this we consider all possible pairwise interactions
       * There are $N^2$ such interactions of which $N$ are self-interactions
       * The probability that a randomly chosen interaction is a
         self-interaction is $p=N/N^{2}=1/N$
       * The probability that if we choose $E$ interactions at random
         $k$ of them will be self-interactions is
	 \[  \Prob{k|E,p} = \mathrm{Binom}(k|E,p) =
         \binom{E}{k}\,p^k\,(1-p)^{E-k} \]
       * In expectation the number of self interactions we would expect
         if they were just random was $E\,p \approx 1.2$ with a
         standard deviation of $\sigma = \sqrt{E\,p\,(1-p)}\approx 1.1$
       * As we have 40 self-interactions this is $32\,\sigma$ above
         the mean, which isn't going to happen by chance!
   - So what do self-interaction do?
   - Most of these self-interactions are inhibitory
   - To understand this we can model the production of a protein with
     and without auto-regulation
     1. /No auto-regulation/
	+ Let's start with a protein, $\hat{X}$, that is produce at
          some rate, $\hat{\alpha}$ and decays at a rate $\hat{\beta}$
	  \[ \varnothing \stackrel{\hat{\beta}}{\longrightarrow} \hat{X} \qquad
           \hat{X} \stackrel{\hat{\alpha}}{\longrightarrow}
          \varnothing \]
	+ The ODE for this process is
	  \[ \frac{\dd [\hat{X}]}{\dd t} = \hat{\beta} -
          \hat{\alpha}\, [\hat{X}] \]
	+ At steady state $\hat{[X]} = \hat{\beta}/\hat{\alpha}$
	+ This is a linear ODE we can actually solve in closed form
          (assuming $[\hat{X}(0)]=0$)
	  \[ [\hat{X}(t)] = \frac{\hat{\beta}}{\hat{\alpha}}
                      \left( 1- \e{-\hat{\alpha}\,t} \right) \]
     2. /Auto-regulation/
	+ We assume that a protein $X$ acts a repressor for itself
	  \[ \frac{\dd [X]}{\dd t} = f([X]) - \alpha \, [X] \]
	+ $f([X])$ is a Hill repressor
	  \[ f([X]) = \frac{\beta}{1 + ([X]/K)^n} \approx
          \beta\,\pred{[X]<K} \]
	+ This is a non-linear equation which we can't solve in closed form
	+ We can easily solve it numerically
	+ We show the dynamics for different $n$ when we use
          $\hat{\beta}=2\beta$  below
	 #+ATTR_LATEX: :width 0.55\textwidth
	 \hspace*{-3cm} [[./figures/autoReg.pdf]]
	+ To compare to the case without auto-regression we scale
          everything so they have the same steady state
	+ We also show the dynamics without auto-regression (green
          line). Note that this is much slower
	+ The blue line is when we replace the Hill repressor with the
          step function
	+ This case is solvable
	  * The dynamics is the same as the linear dynamics when
            $[X]<K$ except we now produce $X$ at a rate $\beta = 2\,\hat{\beta}$
	  * When we reach $[X]=K$ the step switch off the production
            of $X$
	  * The steady state in this case is determined by $K$ (as
            opposed to $\hat{\beta}$ when we have no auto-regulation)
	  * We can make this reaction as fast as we like by increasing $\beta$
	  * This isn't possible for the case without auto-regulation
            because when we increase $\beta$ we change the steady
            state and our time to reach the steady state is just the same
	+ Thus the advantage of negative auto-regulation is that we can speed
          up the time to reach the equilibrium value
	+ A subtler advantage is that $\beta$ and $\alpha$ can
          fluctuate during the life cycle of the cell (for example, as
          the cell increases in size the concentration of $\RNA_X$ may change)
	+ On the other hand, for auto-regulation the value of $K$ is
          depends on the chemical bonds at the binding site for $X$
          which is more stable

* Exercises

** Chemical Equations
   1. Write down the ODE for the following chemical equations
      \begin{align*}
       2\, X + Y  &\stackrel{k_1}{\longrightarrow} Y + Z\\
       Z &\stackrel{k_2}{\longrightarrow} \varnothing
      \end{align*}
   2. And for
       \begin{align*}
       X  &\stackrel{k_1}{\longrightarrow} 2\,X + Z\\
       Z &\stackrel{k_2}{\longrightarrow} 3\,Y
      \end{align*}
 

* Answers

** Chemical Equations
   1. 
       \begin{align*}
       \frac{\dd X}{\dd t} &= -2\,k_1\,[X]^2\,[Y]  &
       \frac{\dd Y}{\dd t} &= 0 &
       \frac{\dd Z}{\dd t} & = k_1\,[X]^2\,[Y]  - k_2 \, [Z]
      \end{align*}
   2. 
      \begin{align*}
      \frac{\dd X}{\dd t} &= k_1\, [X] &
      \frac{\dd Y}{\dd t} &= 3\,k_2 \,[Z] &
      \frac{\dd Z}{\dd t} &=  k_1\, [X] - k_2 \,[Z]
      \end{align*}
